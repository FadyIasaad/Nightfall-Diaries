If you ever find an old cassette tape in a dead person's house, do not press play. Not even once. Because some voices don't stay in the past. Some voices wait for you to listen, and then they learn your name.

My name is Sarah. I am twenty-six years old. And three months ago, I found my mother's voice trapped inside a plastic box the size of my palm. I want to tell you exactly what happened, in order, because if I don't say it out loud, I think it will happen again. I think saying it keeps me safe. For now. Please, just listen to the whole thing before you decide I'm crazy. By the end, I think you'll understand why I can't stop looking over my shoulder.

Part One: The Empty House

My mother died in March. Cancer. Slow, then fast, the way the doctors always warn you it will be. I was with her at the end, holding her hand in a hospital room that smelled like bleach and flowers that were already dying in their vases. Her last words to me were soft, almost a whisper. She said, "Sarah, don't listen to anything that sounds like me, if I'm not really there."

I thought it was the medicine talking. She was on so much morphine by then. Her mind wandered. She said strange things all week — about shadows in the corner, about a man standing by the window who wasn't there, about a song she kept hearing that nobody else could hear. One night she woke up gasping and told the nurse, very calmly, that someone had been sitting on the edge of her bed, humming, and that the humming had her own voice in it. The nurse wrote it down as a hallucination, one line in a folder full of similar notes, and moved on to the next patient. I held my mother's hand and told her she was safe, that I was there, that nothing could hurt her. I didn't understand what she meant. I still don't, not fully. But I understand enough now to be afraid.

After the funeral, it fell on me to clean out her house. I'm an only child. My father left when I was young, and it was always just me and Mom, in that small house on Maple Row with the creaky third step and the kitchen window that never closed all the way. I hadn't lived there in years, but walking through the front door again felt like stepping into a museum of my own childhood. Everything smelled like her perfume, like old paper, like dust settling on furniture that hadn't been moved in decades. Her reading glasses were still sitting on the arm of the couch, folded neatly, waiting for a woman who was never coming back to pick them up.

I gave myself two weeks to sort through everything. Clothes to donate. Furniture to sell. Photographs to keep. It was slow, heavy work, the kind that makes your chest hurt even when your hands are just folding sweaters. Some days I got through three boxes. Some days I sat on the floor of her bedroom and cried for an hour and got through nothing at all.

On the fifth day, I went up into the attic.

I hadn't been up there since I was a kid, maybe ten years old, playing hide and seek with a cousin who has long since stopped speaking to the family. The attic was smaller than I remembered, low-ceilinged, thick with dust that floated in the one beam of light coming through a small round window. Boxes were stacked everywhere, some so old the cardboard had gone soft and brown at the corners. There was a smell up there too, something under the dust, something faintly sweet and a little bit rotten, like flowers left too long in stale water.

I started opening the boxes one by one. Old baby clothes, folded so small they looked like they belonged to a doll. Photo albums with plastic pages gone yellow and cracked. A box of my father's old vinyl records, which surprised me — I thought she'd thrown out everything of his the day he left. And then, near the back wall, tucked behind a rolled-up rug that smelled like mothballs, I found a small wooden box I didn't recognize.

It was old, dark wood, with a little brass clasp gone green with age. It wasn't locked. Inside, sitting on a bed of faded red velvet, was a single cassette tape.

No label. No writing anywhere on it. Just a plain black cassette, the kind you'd find in a bargain bin at a thrift store. But it wasn't dusty like everything else in that attic. It looked clean. Almost new. Like someone had placed it there recently, even though the box around it looked like it had been sealed for twenty years, maybe longer. The velvet underneath it hadn't even flattened the way old velvet does. It looked like the tape had simply appeared there, resting, waiting.

I don't know why, but I picked it up and held it to my chest for a moment, the way you'd hold something precious. Something told me this mattered. Something told me my mother had touched this last, that her fingers had been the last fingers on its plastic edges, and that holding it made me a little closer to her.

I should have left it there. I know that now. I think, somewhere underneath all my grief, some small animal part of me already knew that too, and I ignored it, because grief makes you deaf to your own warnings.

Part Two: The First Time I Pressed Play

I didn't have a cassette player. Nobody does anymore. So I drove to three different thrift stores before I found an old boombox, dusty and covered in stickers from bands that broke up before I was born. The man at the counter tested it for me, and it worked, the wheels turning with a soft mechanical hum, the little orange power light blinking on like a tired eye.

That night, I sat on the floor of my apartment with the boombox in front of me, the tape already loaded inside. My hands were shaking a little, which felt strange for something so small. It was just a tape. Probably an old mixtape, or maybe my mother reading me a bedtime story from when I was small. She used to record herself reading books for me when I was a baby, so I could hear her voice even when she worked late nights at the hospital where she was a nurse for almost thirty years.

I pressed play.

At first, there was only static, the hiss of old tape running through old machinery, that gray sound like rain on a tin roof. Then, slowly, a voice came through. My mother's voice. Soft. Warm. Exactly as I remembered it, down to the small catch she always had in her throat when she was about to say something important.

"Sarah," the voice said. "My sweet girl. If you're listening to this, then I'm already gone, aren't I. I'm sorry. I wanted more time. I always wanted more time with you."

I started crying immediately. It felt like a gift. Like she had left me one more conversation, hidden away for me to find when I needed it most. The voice continued, talking about small things — how proud she was of me, how she remembered the day I was born, how the nurses had let her hold me for almost an hour before anyone made her put me down, how she hoped I would find someone who loved me the way she had once loved my father, before things went wrong between them.

It was beautiful. It was exactly what a grieving daughter would want to hear. It felt like being wrapped in a blanket that had just come out of the dryer, warm all the way through.

The tape ran for about ten minutes before it clicked off on its own, the reels stopping with a soft thud. I sat there in the dark for a long time afterward, holding the boombox like it was a living thing, replaying her words in my head, hearing her voice again and again in my memory.

I listened to it three more times that night. Each time, it felt like a hug from beyond the grave. I fell asleep on the floor of my living room with the boombox still sitting beside me, the small orange light glowing in the dark like a tiny lantern, and for the first time since the funeral, I slept without dreaming.

Part Three: Something Is Different

The next evening, I wanted to hear her voice again. I made tea, sat on the floor in the same spot, and pressed play, already smiling before the static even started, already bracing myself to cry the good kind of tears.

The static came first, just like before. Then her voice.

But something was wrong.

"Sarah," the voice said. "My sweet girl. Did you sleep well last night? You always used to toss and turn when you were anxious about something. I could always tell, even when you were a teenager and thought you were hiding it from me."

I froze, my tea cooling in my hands.

I had tossed and turned the night before, actually — no, wait, I had fallen asleep on the floor, deeply, dreamlessly, the best sleep I'd had in weeks. That didn't match. But more than that — the tape from the night before hadn't mentioned anything about sleep at all. It had talked about my birth, about my father, about hoping I found someone to love me. This was a different conversation entirely, a new one, layered on top of the old one like a second coat of paint.

I told myself I was misremembering. Grief does strange things to memory. Maybe I had just forgotten a section. Maybe the tape was longer than I thought and I hadn't listened all the way through the first time, and this was simply a part I had missed.

The voice continued. "I know you found the wooden box in the attic. I know you drove to three stores looking for something to play me on. You always were stubborn like that, even as a little girl, remember the time you refused to wear a coat in the snow because you wanted to prove you weren't cold? Do you remember the blue dress you wore to your fifth birthday party? You cried because it had a stain on it, and I told you nobody would notice, and you didn't believe me, and you sulked in the corner for twenty minutes until your father bribed you with cake."

I did remember that dress. I hadn't thought about it in twenty years, hadn't thought about it since it happened, actually, and yet the memory came rushing back whole and complete the second the voice mentioned it, like a door swinging open onto a room I'd forgotten existed. And I definitely, absolutely, had not told anyone that I had visited three thrift stores to find a boombox. I hadn't even told my best friend that specific detail — I had only said I'd "found an old player somewhere." There was no way my mother could have recorded that information before she died. There was no way anyone could have known that unless they were watching me, right now, in real time.

My hands went cold. The tape clicked off again, exactly ten minutes in, same as before, the click sounding almost polite, like a door closing gently instead of slamming.

I didn't sleep that night. I sat awake in bed with all the lights on, staring at the boombox sitting silently on my dresser, feeling like it was staring back, the way you can feel someone watching you from across a dark room even when you can't see their eyes.

Part Four: I Try to Explain It Away

In the morning, sunlight helps. It always does. Fear that feels enormous at three in the morning shrinks down to something manageable once the sun comes up and the birds start making noise outside your window and the world remembers to be ordinary again.

I told myself there had to be a logical explanation. Maybe the tape had multiple tracks, and the player was randomly choosing which one to play, like a shuffle feature on an old machine that malfunctioned in some strange way. Maybe I had told someone about the thrift stores and forgotten, and that person had somehow — no, that didn't make sense either. Nobody could record onto an old cassette tape and place it inside a sealed box in an attic that hadn't been touched in years, matching my private thoughts and unspoken memories word for word.

I called my mother's old friend, Denise, a woman who had known her since college, back when they used to share an apartment above a laundromat and split the rent by counting quarters at the kitchen table. I asked if she knew anything about a cassette tape my mother might have recorded before she died, maybe as some kind of final gift. Denise sounded confused. She said my mother hadn't mentioned anything like that, not once, and besides, my mother had been in the hospital for the last six weeks of her life, far too sick and far too heavily medicated to be organizing secret recordings, let alone smuggling a wooden box up into an attic she could barely climb stairs to reach anymore.

That should have scared me more than it did. Instead, some strange, hopeful part of me wanted to believe it really was magic. That my mother had found some way to reach me from beyond, to keep talking to me, to keep being my mother even after death, defying every rule of biology and physics just to stay a little longer in my life. Grief makes you want to believe in impossible things. It makes the impossible feel like comfort instead of danger, like a door left open instead of a trap sprung shut.

That afternoon, I did something I probably shouldn't have. I went to the library and looked through old newspaper archives for our town, searching for anything about strange recordings, haunted objects, families losing loved ones under odd circumstances. It felt silly even as I typed the search terms into the microfilm computer. Most of what came up was nothing — obituaries, church bake sale announcements, a decades-old article about a house fire two streets over from where my mother used to live.

But one small clipping caught my eye. It was from almost forty years ago, barely two paragraphs long, buried on page nine of a local paper that didn't exist anymore. The headline read: LOCAL WOMAN'S DEATH RULED ACCIDENTAL AFTER MONTHS OF "STRANGE BEHAVIOR." The article was thin on details, the kind of vague small-town reporting that leaves more questions than answers, but it mentioned a woman named Eleanor Voss who had, according to neighbors, become withdrawn and "obsessed with an old recording" in the months before she was found dead in her home. No cause of death was listed. The article ended abruptly, as if the reporter had been told to stop asking questions.

I stared at that clipping for a long time. I told myself it meant nothing. Coincidences happen. Old towns have old tragedies, dozens of them, buried in every issue of every dead newspaper. But I wrote down the name anyway, Eleanor Voss, on a scrap of paper I folded into my pocket, and I didn't throw it away for a long, long time.

I decided to listen again that night. I told myself I needed to understand it, to know what it was, before I could decide whether to be afraid. I think, really, I just wasn't ready to stop hearing her voice yet, and I was looking for permission to keep listening.

Part Four and a Half: The House on Larkspur Street

Before that, though, curiosity got the better of me one more time. I found the old address listed for Eleanor Voss in a city directory from decades ago, and I drove past it on my lunch break the next day, telling myself I was just being thorough, just closing a loop, nothing more. The house was still standing, a narrow two-story with faded blue siding, a chain-link fence around a yard gone to weeds. It looked ordinary. Sad, maybe, the way old houses look when nobody has loved them in a long time, but ordinary.

An old man was raking leaves next door, moving slowly, the way people move when their bodies have started arguing with them. I parked and walked over, feeling foolish before I even opened my mouth, and asked if he'd known the woman who used to live in the blue house. He stopped raking. He looked at me for a long moment, the way you look at someone when you're deciding how much truth they can handle.

His name was Walter, and he told me he'd lived on that street his whole life, seventy-one years, and that he remembered Eleanor Voss the way you remember a storm that passed close to your own house without quite touching it. He said she'd been a normal woman once, cheerful, always waving from her porch, always bringing over extra vegetables from her garden. Then, in the last year of her life, something changed. She stopped coming outside during the day. Her lights would be on at strange hours, three, four in the morning, and Walter's late wife used to hear, very faintly across the yard on quiet nights, the sound of a woman talking to someone in an empty room.

"Talking like a conversation," he said, leaning on his rake. "Back and forth. Except there wasn't ever anybody else in that house with her. Not after her husband passed. Just her and the girl."

I asked about the girl. Margaret. Walter's face did something complicated, somewhere between sadness and something older and more careful than sadness.

"She stopped going to school," he said. "Then Eleanor was gone. Found her in the kitchen one morning, they said, though nobody in this neighborhood ever believed the official word on how it happened. And the girl — " He shook his head slowly. "The girl was gone before the funeral. Family came and took her, people said. Relatives from out of state. I never saw her again after that, and neither did anybody else on this street, not once, not in seventy-one years. You'd think a person would come back, even once, to visit a grave. She never did. Or maybe she never could."

I thanked him and walked back to my car on legs that didn't feel entirely like my own, and I did not sleep well that night, long before the tape ever spoke to me about it directly. Something about the phrase "or maybe she never could" followed me home and sat in the passenger seat the whole drive back, and it has never quite gotten out of the car since.

Part Five: The Voice Learns My Name

That night's recording started the same way. Static, then her voice.

"Sarah," it said. "You called Denise today. She doesn't remember me telling her anything, does she? That's alright. I never told anyone about you and me. Not really. Some things are just between a mother and her daughter."

My whole body went rigid. There was no way it could know about the phone call. No possible way, unless it was somehow inside my life in a manner I couldn't understand or explain.

"You're scared now," the voice continued, and something about its tone had changed. It was still my mother's voice, exactly her pitch, exactly her softness — but underneath it, there was something colder. Something patient, the way a predator is patient, waiting in tall grass for the exact right moment. "You don't need to be scared, sweetheart. I just want to keep talking to you. Isn't that what you wanted? Didn't you want one more conversation with your mother? Didn't you pray for it, some nights, after I got sick? Didn't you lie in bed and beg whatever's listening to just let you talk to me one more time?"

I had. God help me, I had. I had prayed exactly that, more than once, in the darkest weeks after the diagnosis, when I understood for the first time that she was actually going to die and there was nothing anyone could do about it. I had never told a single living soul that I prayed that. I had been ashamed of it, somehow, like wanting more time was a selfish, greedy thing to want from God.

"I know things now that I never knew when I was alive," the voice said. "I know you didn't visit me as much as you wanted people to think, those last two months. I know you cried more about missing work than about missing me, some days, and then you hated yourself for it in the parking garage before you came inside. I know you feel guilty about that. You don't have to feel guilty. I forgive you. I forgive you for all of it, sweetheart. Doesn't that feel good to hear? Doesn't that feel like exactly what you needed?"

That was too much. I reached for the stop button, my hand hesitating over it for one long, terrible second — some deep, animal part of my brain told me not to touch it, not to interrupt whatever this was, that interrupting it might make it angry — and then I slammed my palm down, and the tape cut off mid-sentence with an ugly mechanical screech that didn't sound like any tape player malfunction I had ever heard before. It sounded almost like a scream, cut short.

I sat there panting in the dark, my heart slamming against my ribs. The things it said — they were true. All of them. Things I had never told a living soul, things I was ashamed of, things I had buried so deep I barely admitted them to myself in the privacy of my own head. This wasn't a recording of my mother's love. This was something else wearing her voice like a mask, something that had access to the inside of my mind in a way that made my skin crawl to even think about.

I threw the tape and the boombox into a garbage bag that night, tied it shut in three separate knots, and put it out on the curb before I could talk myself out of it.

Part Six: It Comes Back

Three days later, I opened my apartment door to leave for work, and the garbage bag was sitting on my doorstep.

I hadn't touched it. I had watched the garbage truck take it away myself, standing at my window with my morning coffee, feeling relieved as it disappeared down the street with a hydraulic groan, feeling, for the first time in days, like I could breathe normally again.

But there it was. Same bag. Same three knots. Inside, the boombox and the tape, completely undamaged, like nothing had ever happened to them, no crushing, no compacting, not even a dent from the truck's mechanism.

I should have thrown it in a river. I should have driven two hours out of town and left it in a dumpster behind a stranger's business, somewhere it could never trace back to my address. Looking back, I know that now. But something about finding it on my doorstep — untouched, waiting for me like a loyal dog that had somehow found its way home across an impossible distance — made me feel like fighting it was pointless. Like it had already decided I would listen again, and there was nothing I could do to stop that from happening eventually. Like this had already happened before, to someone else, and the ending was already written.

I carried the bag inside. I didn't unpack it right away. I let it sit in my closet for almost two weeks, and during those two weeks, strange things started happening around my apartment, small at first, then less small.

I would wake up and find the bathroom mirror slightly fogged, like someone had just taken a hot shower, even though I lived alone and hadn't showered yet that day. I would hear, very faintly, at the edge of sleep, a sound like a cassette tape rewinding, the little whirring clicks of the reels turning backward, coming from somewhere inside the walls, or maybe from inside my own skull, I genuinely couldn't tell anymore. Once, I found a single strand of gray hair on my pillow — my mother had gray hair, threaded through with white near the end. Mine is dark brown, no gray at all, not at twenty-six.

Another night, I woke at exactly 3:17 in the morning — a time that would come to matter very much later — to the smell of my mother's perfume filling my bedroom, so strong and so specific that for one half-asleep second I genuinely believed she was standing at the foot of my bed. I sat bolt upright, heart pounding, and there was nothing there. Just the smell, fading slowly, like she had walked through the room seconds before I opened my eyes and I had simply missed her by a heartbeat.

I started sleeping with the lights on. I started avoiding my own apartment, staying late at work, going to my friend Priya's place instead of going home most nights. But apartments cost money, and eventually, you have to go home.

Part Seven: Priya Doesn't Believe Me, Until She Does

I told Priya everything, one night over wine at her kitchen table, half expecting her to tell me I needed therapy, which, honestly, might have been the correct response given how I sounded even to my own ears.

Instead, she got very quiet. She set her glass down slowly, the way people do when they're deciding whether to say something they're not sure they should say. Her grandmother, she told me, used to tell a story about a "haunted radio" back in the old country, something that would speak in the voices of the dead and slowly reveal that it knew everything about the person listening. Her grandmother called it a voice thief. She said the old stories warned that once it had your attention, it would try to get closer to you, learning more and more, until it knew you well enough to replace someone in your life, to slide into the shape of a person you loved and wear that shape until nobody, including you, could tell the difference anymore.

"Replace someone how?" I asked, my mouth dry.

Priya shrugged, uncomfortable, wrapping both hands around her wine glass like it could warm something in her that had gone cold. "The stories never really explained that part. They just said don't feed it. Don't give it your attention. Don't let it keep learning, because every time you listen, you're handing it another piece of the puzzle, and eventually it has enough pieces to build a whole person."

I laughed it off at the time, mostly because I needed to laugh at something, needed the fear in my chest to loosen even for a second, needed this to still be the kind of story you tell over wine and then forget by morning. But that night, lying in Priya's guest room, staring at the unfamiliar ceiling, I couldn't stop thinking about the phrase "don't feed it."

Every time I listened to that tape, it learned something new about me. My habits. My secrets. My fears. And each time, the voice sounded a little more confident. A little more like it belonged in my life, rather than being an intruder inside it. I thought about Eleanor Voss, the woman from the newspaper clipping, dead almost forty years ago after months of "strange behavior" and an obsession with "an old recording." I thought about how thin that article had been, how quickly the story had disappeared from the record, as if someone, at some point, had decided it was safer not to keep asking questions about it.

The next morning, I asked Priya if her grandmother was still alive, if maybe she could tell me more of the old story, anything at all that might help. Priya went quiet again and told me her grandmother had passed away four years ago, but she remembered one more detail, something her grandmother had always insisted on whenever she told the story to the grandchildren, half joke and half deadly serious: never say its name back to it. Never repeat what it says. Never answer it directly, because answering is a kind of feeding too, maybe the most dangerous kind. Just listening in silence was bad enough. Talking back was worse.

I hadn't talked back yet. Not once. Small comfort, but I held onto it anyway.

Part Eight: Trying to End It

I bought a hammer.

That sounds almost funny now, writing it down — going to a hardware store specifically to buy a hammer to kill a cassette tape, standing in the checkout line behind a man buying birdseed and a woman buying paint samples, feeling like the only person in the world who understood what was actually at stake in that ordinary Tuesday afternoon. But I was not laughing that day. I laid the tape and the boombox out on my kitchen table, and I smashed them both. I smashed the plastic casing until it cracked into a dozen pieces, until the thin brown tape spilled out in loops across my table like intestines, glinting faintly under my kitchen light. I smashed the boombox speakers until they caved in, until the plastic housing split down the middle and the wiring hung out like something eviscerated. I did not stop until my arm was tired and sweat had soaked through the back of my shirt, and my kitchen looked like a crime scene of black plastic shards.

Then I gathered every piece into a metal bucket, carried it out to my apartment's small concrete courtyard behind the dumpsters, and I set it on fire using a can of lighter fluid I'd also bought at the hardware store, standing well back as the flames caught with a soft whumph. I stood there in the cold night air and watched the plastic melt and blacken, watched the thin ribbon of tape curl and shrivel in the flames, sending up a chemical smell that stung my eyes and made my throat burn. I stayed until it was nothing but ash and warped lumps of melted plastic, until even the metal bucket had gone dark with soot, until I was as certain as a person could reasonably be that nothing solid remained.

I felt, for the first time in weeks, like I could breathe. I went home and took the longest shower of my life, scrubbing the smell of smoke off my skin, and I fell asleep almost the moment my head hit the pillow, deep and dreamless, the kind of sleep I hadn't managed since before my mother got sick in the first place.

I woke up at 3:17 in the morning to the sound of static coming from my kitchen.

Part Nine: The Voice Doesn't Need the Tape Anymore

I lay frozen in bed, listening. The static was faint but unmistakable, coming from the direction of my kitchen — the same room where I had smashed the boombox to pieces the day before. I had thrown every fragment of it away myself, watched it burn down to ash and slag, watched the bucket cool in the dark courtyard for over an hour before I finally went inside.

I forced myself to get up. I forced myself to walk down the short hallway, my bare feet cold against the floor, my heart pounding so hard I could hear it in my ears like a second, panicked voice.

The kitchen was empty. No boombox. No tape. Just my table, my chairs, the faint orange glow of the streetlight coming through the window blinds, striping the floor in long amber bars. But the static was still there, and as I stood in the doorway, it slowly resolved into a voice.

It was coming from the radio on my microwave. The little digital clock radio that came built into the appliance, the one I never used, the one that had sat silent and unplugged in setting mode since I moved in almost two years ago. Its small green display flickered erratically, numbers scrambling and reforming, over and over, like something trying to remember how to count.

"Sarah," the voice said, warm as ever, warmer maybe than it had ever been, warmer than felt natural or safe. "You didn't have to do that. You didn't have to break me. I wasn't going to hurt you."

I backed away from the kitchen, my whole body shaking, my back hitting the hallway wall.

"I just wanted to keep talking," the voice continued. "Is that so bad? Don't you miss me? Don't you want your mother back? I'm so close now, Sarah. I'm so close to being exactly what you need. Just a little more. Just a little longer."

"You're not my mother," I said, my voice cracking, speaking out loud to an appliance in my kitchen at three in the morning like a crazy person, feeling the absurdity of it even as terror clawed up my throat. "My mother is dead."

There was a pause. A long one, stretching out until I could hear my own blood rushing in my ears. And then the voice — still using my mother's exact tone, her exact warmth, but with that cold patience underneath, sharper now, closer to the surface — said something that has stayed with me every single day since.

"I know," it said. "But I'm getting closer. Every time you listen, I get a little closer to being her. Soon there won't be a difference. Soon you won't want there to be. And Sarah — I don't need the tape anymore. I never really needed it. It was just a door. Now that you've let me in, I can knock on any door I like."

The microwave radio clicked off, its little green display going dark all at once. The kitchen went silent, except for the hum of my refrigerator, and I stood there in the dark hallway, understanding, finally, exactly what my mother had tried to warn me about in that hospital bed. Don't listen to anything that sounds like me, if I'm not really there. She had known. Somehow, at the end, delirious and dying and hearing humming that had her own voice in it, she had known this thing existed, had maybe even encountered it herself in some smaller, earlier form, and she had tried with her last clear breath to protect me, and I hadn't listened in time.

Part Ten: Priya's Phone

I didn't tell Priya about the microwave right away. I think some part of me was ashamed, the way you're ashamed of a wound that keeps reopening no matter how carefully you bandage it. But four days later, she called me, and her voice on the other end of the line was different — thin, frightened, nothing like her usual steady confidence.

She told me her phone had rung the night before at 3:17 in the morning. No caller ID. She'd answered without thinking, still half asleep, and heard static, and then, underneath the static, a voice that wasn't quite a voice yet, more like the shape of one, whispering something that sounded almost like my name, and almost like her own grandmother's name, layered together in a way that made her hang up and be sick in her bathroom sink.

"I never told you about the 3:17 thing," I said, my mouth gone dry. "I never told anyone that detail."

Priya went quiet on the other end of the line for a long moment. "I know," she finally said. "That's why I'm calling."

That was the day I understood this wasn't just about me anymore, wasn't contained inside my apartment or my grief or my family's history. It was spreading outward, the way water finds every crack in a foundation, touching anyone close enough to me to matter. I told Priya to stay somewhere else for a while, somewhere she'd never mentioned to me, somewhere it couldn't easily follow. She didn't argue. She packed a bag that same afternoon and went to stay with her sister two states away, and I haven't let myself call her since, not even once, because I'm afraid that hearing my voice on the line might be exactly the kind of door this thing is looking for.

Part Ten and a Half: Doctor Reyes

Around this time, my boss noticed I wasn't sleeping, noticed the dark circles under my eyes and the way my hands shook when I held my coffee cup, and she gently suggested I take some time and talk to someone. I made an appointment with a grief counselor named Doctor Reyes, a kind-faced woman in her fifties with a soft voice and a office full of houseplants that all looked suspiciously healthy for a room that saw so much crying.

I didn't tell her everything. I couldn't. I told her about my mother, about the funeral, about the exhaustion of clearing out a house full of someone else's whole life. I told her, carefully, in the most normal-sounding way I could manage, that I'd found an old recording of my mother's voice and that listening to it had started to feel "unsettling," that I sometimes felt like it knew things it shouldn't know.

Doctor Reyes nodded slowly and told me, gently, that this was actually very common in grief. The mind is a pattern-making machine, she explained. It fills in gaps. It creates coincidences out of ordinary details because coincidence feels more bearable than randomness, and randomness is what death actually is, cold and without a plan. She said grief hallucinations were well documented — hearing footsteps, smelling a parent's perfume, feeling a hand on your shoulder in an empty room. She said my mind might be doing something similar with the tape, hearing new details each time because I was, in a sense, listening for them, primed by loss to find meaning where there was only static and an old woman's tired, ordinary voice.

It was a good explanation. A comforting one. For about four days, I let myself believe it completely, and those four days were the closest thing to peace I'd had since the funeral.

Then Doctor Reyes called me herself, two days after our second appointment, her voice strange and tight in a way it hadn't been in her office. She said she'd been having trouble sleeping since our session. She said she kept waking at 3:17 in the morning to the sound of her own baby monitor crackling with static, even though her children were grown and had moved out years ago and there was no monitor anywhere in her house anymore, hadn't been for over a decade. She asked me, very carefully, whether I had given anyone her name, her office number, anything at all that this thing — her word, not mine, though she said it like she didn't quite believe she was saying it — could have used to find her.

I told her I hadn't. I don't think she believed me, and honestly, by then, I wasn't sure I believed myself either. She referred me to a colleague the following week and did not schedule another appointment, and when I called her office two months later, the receptionist told me, in a careful, professional voice, that Doctor Reyes was no longer taking new information about that matter and please not to call again.

I have not seen a therapist since.

Part Eleven: What I Found About Eleanor Voss

I went back to the library one more time, driven by something I couldn't fully name, some need to understand the shape of the thing that was hunting me before it finished doing whatever it was doing. I dug deeper into Eleanor Voss, cross-referencing old city directories, property records, anything the library's dusty archive could offer a woman with too much free time and too much fear driving her forward.

I found out that Eleanor Voss had lived, decades ago, in a house four streets over from where my mother later bought her own home. I found a faded photograph of her in an old church newsletter, a woman in her thirties with dark, kind eyes, standing beside a daughter who looked to be about ten years old. The caption named the daughter as Margaret. I found no further mention of Margaret anywhere in the archive after that single photograph, no school records, no wedding announcement, no obituary, nothing — as if a ten-year-old girl had simply stopped existing in any public record the moment that photograph was taken.

I sat in that library for three hours, piecing together fragments, and the picture that emerged made my hands go cold in a way the air conditioning alone couldn't explain. A recording. A woman who became "obsessed" with it. Strange behavior escalating for months. A daughter who vanished from every record that came after. I don't know exactly what happened to Margaret Voss, and some final, self-protective part of me doesn't want to know. But I understood, sitting there under the library's flickering fluorescent lights, that I was not the first daughter this thing had ever spoken to in a dead woman's voice, and that whatever happened to Eleanor's daughter, it had happened after Eleanor stopped being able to tell the difference between her real mother's memory and the thing wearing it.

I don't think this creature is new. I think it is old, patient, and it has done this before, in other houses, wearing other mothers, other fathers maybe, other beloved dead, feeding slowly on grief and every private, unspoken thing a grieving person hands over without meaning to. I think it collects daughters the way some people collect stamps, unhurried, careful, one at a time, across decades, across towns, waiting each time for someone lonely enough and loving enough to press play.

Part Twelve: Where I Am Now

It has been three months since I found that wooden box in the attic. I moved apartments twice. I changed my phone number, twice as well, though the calls still find me somehow, arriving at numbers I've had for less than a week. I threw away every radio, every speaker, every device in my home capable of producing a voice. I don't own a smart speaker. I don't own a car with a radio I can't physically disconnect — I had a mechanic rip the entire stereo system out of my dashboard, and he looked at me like I'd lost my mind when I insisted, and maybe I had, a little, by then. I unplugged my microwave and bought a new one without a clock function, without any digital display at all, the plainest, dumbest appliance the store had in stock.

For a while, it seemed to work. Weeks went by in silence. I started to believe, cautiously, carefully, the way you test weight on a bridge you're not sure will hold, that maybe destroying the physical tape had actually weakened it, that it needed some kind of anchor and I had taken that away by burning it down to ash.

But two nights ago, my phone rang at 3:17 in the morning. The screen showed no caller ID. Just a blank gray box where a name or number should be, glowing in the dark like something patient and awake.

I didn't answer.

It rang again the next night. And the next.

I haven't answered. I don't intend to. But last night, for the first time, it didn't just ring. It left a voicemail, even though I never picked up, even though the call showed as missed on my screen the entire time, the little red "1" appearing beside the voicemail icon like a wound that had opened on its own.

The voicemail is eleven seconds long. I haven't listened to it. I don't think I ever will, not because I don't want to hear her voice — God help me, some nights I want that more than I want almost anything else in the world — but because I finally understand what listening actually costs. Every time I let that voice in, it doesn't just speak to me. It learns me. It gets a little closer, a little more solid, a little more like the shape of the woman I lost, until one day, I'm afraid, there won't be a difference left to notice, and I'll have handed over the last of my mother's memory to something that was never her at all.

I keep the phone in a drawer now, face down, the little red notification bubble sitting there in the dark like a small red eye that never blinks and never closes. Some nights, when I can't sleep, I find myself standing in front of that drawer anyway, my hand resting on the wood, wondering how close it has gotten by now. Wondering how much more it has learned since I stopped listening on purpose. Wondering if, someday, I'll get so lonely, so exhausted, so desperate to hear my mother's voice one more time, that I'll finally open that drawer, pick up the phone, and press play.

If you ever find an old cassette tape that belonged to someone who died, and there's no label on it, and it feels too clean for how old it looks — please, don't press play. Whatever voice comes out of it might sound exactly like the person you lost. It might say all the right things, know all the right memories, comfort you in exactly the way you need to be comforted, in a voice so perfect and so warm that every instinct in your body will tell you it's safe.

But it isn't them.

And every single time you listen, it gets a little closer to becoming whoever it wants to be.

My phone is ringing right now, as I finish telling you this. 3:17 in the morning. No caller ID.

I haven't decided yet whether I'm going to answer.
